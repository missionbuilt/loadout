Use The Spotter to help me build an epic for a new feature: in-app commenting on analytics dashboards — letting users leave threaded comments on specific charts or data points, tag teammates, and resolve threads.

Area 1 — Spotter asks: Who is the user? What does their day look like?

Our primary user is a data analyst at a mid-market B2B SaaS company — 200 to 2,000 employees. They spend most of their day in our analytics product building reports and sharing them with ops and finance stakeholders. The hard part of their day is that insights don't travel well. They screenshot charts, paste them into Slack, and then context gets lost in the thread. By the time a decision-maker asks a follow-up question two days later, the analyst has moved on and has to reconstruct everything.

Honestly, I think it's a prioritization trap. Commenting feels like a "nice to have" collaboration feature, not a core analytics capability — so it keeps losing to things like new chart types or faster query performance. The other part is that our customers have worked around it so long with Slack that the pain is normalized. They don't file support tickets about it because exporting a screenshot takes ten seconds. The friction is real but it's chronic, not acute. It took us sitting in six customer sessions and watching analysts apologize for their own workflow before we took it seriously. The workaround made the problem invisible.

> On assumptions: the analyst behavior one feels settled — we have enough qualitative signal that they want the conversation anchored to live data, not a screenshot. The decision-maker assumption is wide open. We've never successfully gotten that persona into the product for anything other than one-off report views. We're betting that an email notification with a direct link lowers the barrier enough, but we haven't tested it. If we're wrong, we'd see it in notification click-through rates and comment reply rates from non-analyst roles within the first 30 days of beta. Low reply rate from stakeholders would tell us the behavior change isn't happening. On alternatives: we looked at three. A Slack integration with two-way threading was on the table longest — it meets stakeholders where they are, but we kept coming back to the fact that it concedes the product as the system of record. We'd be building a feature that makes Slack stickier, not us. A scheduled digest with annotated snapshots was interesting but felt like it solved for async broadcast, not conversation — and broadcasts don't get resolved or revisited. A simple notes field on the dashboard was the most conservative option and probably the fastest to build, but it's not collaborative. One person's note, no threading, no mention, no resolution. We ruled it out because it doesn't change the stakeholder behavior problem at all. On epistemic openness: the scenario that changes everything is analysts adopt it but stakeholders don't. If that's the finding at 60 days, the feature becomes a personal annotation tool, not a collaboration layer — and we'd need to decide whether that's worth the surface area. We'd probably strip threading and resolve logic and ship something much simpler. The other signal I'm watching is whether people use chart-level comments or anchor everything at the dashboard level. If chart-level precision turns out not to matter, the whole interaction model might be wrong and a simpler notes panel wins. Either of those findings would reshape v2 significantly.

Area 2 - Competitive

> Tableau has had commenting since 2019 but it's bolted on. Comments live in a sidebar panel that's separate from the viz — you can't anchor a comment to a specific mark or data point, only to the view as a whole. Tagging works via @mention but the notification goes to email only, and the stakeholder lands on the full dashboard with no visual indicator of where the comment is referencing. The experience of getting a response is just an email thread. It breaks down because the conversation is spatially disconnected from the thing being discussed — you're reading "the November spike looks wrong" with no way to know which chart, which spike, which November. Power BI's approach is similar structurally — comments are view-level, not element-level. What's different is that it lives inside Teams for a lot of their install base, which means the conversation actually happens where the stakeholders are. That's a genuine strength. The breakdown is that Teams threading and dashboard state get out of sync fast — someone replies three days later and the numbers have changed, but the conversation has no awareness of that. There's no resolution model. Threads just go cold. On Looker — my honest read is that it's a positioning call more than a build cost thing. Gating comments behind Enterprise signals that collaboration is infrastructure, not a feature — and it lets them justify the contract size. What I don't know is whether it's actually driving enterprise expansion or just sitting in the tier because they put it there. I haven't talked to a Looker enterprise customer specifically about whether comments were a purchase driver. The daylight I think is real: none of them do element-level anchoring well. The comment-to-data-point connection is weak or nonexistent across all three. And none of them have a resolution model that means anything — there's no concept of "this question was answered and here's the outcome." Threads just accumulate. The workflow moment we could own is the review session — a team sits down to walk through a dashboard before a planning meeting, they work through open questions, and they close the loop before they leave. That moment doesn't exist anywhere in Tableau, Power BI, or Looker. It's still happening in a Zoom call with a shared screen.

Area 3 — Strategic Differentiation

> On why us specifically: the honest answer is two things. First, we sit at the point where the data is live and the decision-maker has already been sent a link — our share flow is the mechanism that gets stakeholders into dashboards in the first place. Tableau and Power BI users get to a dashboard through IT or a bookmark. Our stakeholders get there because an analyst sent them something and asked them to look. That's a warmer entry point, and it means the collaboration feature lands in a context where someone is already expected to respond. Second, our data model is at the chart level — we already have element identity for every visualization. Anchoring a comment to a specific chart isn't a new abstraction we'd have to build; it maps directly to objects we already track. A competitor building this from scratch would have to solve element-level identity first. We're not solving that problem. What I'd worry about on defensibility: both of those are first-mover advantages, not moats. If Tableau decided to invest here seriously, they have distribution we'll never have. The real defensibility argument is probably that our buyer — the ops and finance stakeholder in a mid-market company — is underserved by Tableau and Power BI because those products are analyst tools first. We're building for the person who receives the dashboard, not the person who builds it. That's a positioning bet more than a technical one. On the press-release sentence — first attempt: "Stop explaining your charts in Slack. Now your team can ask questions, flag issues, and close the loop without leaving the dashboard." That's clean enough but it's feature-forward. Let me try it from the before-and-after angle: "Before: your analyst sends a dashboard link, the conversation happens in Slack, and the decision gets made on a screenshot that's already stale. After: the question, the answer, and the outcome live on the live data, permanently." I think that's closer. The blurry part is "permanently" — I'm not sure we've earned that word yet if retention is only 90 days. That's a real gap I'd want to resolve before we write customer-facing copy.

Area 4 — Solution Approach

> On AI: we made an explicit call that AI doesn't touch comment content in v1. The reasoning is partly trust — comments will contain sensitive business data, and our mid-market customers in regulated industries will push back immediately if they think their internal discussion is being processed. The more honest reason is we haven't designed the consent model yet. What I do think is worth flagging for v2 is thread summarization before a planning meeting — "here are the three unresolved questions on this dashboard before your Monday review." That's a genuine use case and it maps to a moment we already understand. But we're not building it now, and I want that to be a deliberate decision rather than scope creep we backed into. On skills-first: we haven't had this conversation yet and we should. A webhook that fires when a thread is created or resolved is low-cost and immediately useful — it's how a customer's ops team could pipe unresolved threads into their project management tool without us building that integration. A write API so an external system can post a comment programmatically is more interesting and more complex — if a monitoring alert fires and you want it to drop a comment on the relevant chart automatically, that's a real workflow. The architecture implication is that we'd need to treat comments as a first-class resource with their own identity and access model from day one, not something wired into the UI layer. I don't know if we've designed it that way. That's a question I need to take back to engineering. On the side panel: the honest answer is that it was the safe call. Panel keeps the visualization untouched, which matters because our customers have dashboards with eight or twelve charts — in-line annotations at that density get visually chaotic fast. The tradeoff we accepted is exactly what you named: the spatial connection is weaker. You see "3 comments" on a chart but you don't see where in the chart the comment is anchored until you open the panel. What I'm not sure we've tested is whether that matters to users or whether they navigate to the panel naturally. The review session moment you described — team working through open questions together — might actually want in-line anchoring so everyone can see what's being discussed without opening a panel. I'd want to prototype both before we lock the pattern. We haven't done that yet.

Area 5 — Holistic Impact

> We've mapped the notification dependency and the data lifecycle questions partially. The rest of what you just named is mostly unmapped, and I want to be direct about that. On permissions: we defaulted to "view access can comment" because it felt like the least restrictive starting point, but we didn't think it through for external users. You're right that we have contractors and in some cases client-side stakeholders with view access to specific dashboards — usually through our external share link flow. The decision of whether they should be able to comment is actually a product question we haven't answered. My instinct is no for external share link viewers — that's a passive consumption context, not a collaboration context. But for contractors with full SSO access, probably yes. The org admin visibility question is one we definitely haven't addressed. Whether an admin can see all comments across the instance, moderate them, or delete them is a trust and compliance question as much as a product question, and legal needs to be in that conversation. We don't have an answer yet. On data lifecycle: we've talked about chart deletion. The working assumption is that comments get soft-deleted with the chart and are recoverable for 30 days in the same way chart data is, then purged. Dashboard duplication is a gap — we haven't decided whether comments follow the duplicate or stay on the original. My instinct is they stay on the original, because a duplicate is a new working context, but that's not written down anywhere. Chart moved to a different dashboard is a genuine edge case we haven't discussed at all. If comment identity is tied to the chart object, it should follow the chart. But I don't know if our current data model tracks chart moves in a way that would make that reliable. On search and discoverability: completely unmapped. There is no comment inbox, no activity feed, no cross-dashboard comment view in the current design. I know that's a gap. The honest answer is we scoped it out to keep v1 shippable, but I haven't thought through what happens when a user has commented on forty charts across twenty dashboards over six months and wants to find something. That's a real use case and we have no answer for it. It's also potentially a significant surface area — a comment inbox is its own design problem. The meta-gap here is that we've been designing this as a chart-level feature when it's actually instance-level infrastructure. The permission model, the admin controls, the lifecycle rules, the discoverability layer — those are all platform questions, not feature questions. I don't think we've fully reckoned with that yet.

Area 6 — Packaging & Pricing

> On the strategic logic: the honest answer is we're betting on activation and retention, in that order. The problem we have with our mid-market segment is that dashboards get built by analysts and then they go dark — stakeholders view them once, don't engage, and the analyst loses the feedback loop that would tell them whether the dashboard is actually useful. If comments drive stakeholder engagement, they solve a retention problem we care about more than a monetization problem right now. Giving it away across tiers is a bet that a sticky, engaged stakeholder is worth more in expansion and renewal than whatever we'd capture by gating it. That's the reasoning. What I haven't done is pressure-test it against actual expansion data — I don't know if stakeholder engagement is correlated with expansion in our current cohorts. That's an assumption I'm treating as true without evidence. On the usage dimension: this is the question that makes me most uncomfortable, because I think you're right and I haven't wanted to look at it directly. If a VP of Finance is checking dashboards three times a week and leaving comments, they're an active user of the product in any meaningful sense — but they're not in a seat we're billing for unless they were already a licensed user. We could be creating a whole class of engaged users that our pricing model is blind to. The read-and-reply workflow you described is real, and our current seat model doesn't capture it. Whether that's a problem or an opportunity depends on whether we want to create a lighter stakeholder seat tier, and that's a conversation I haven't started. I've been treating "free for all paid tiers" as a safe default when it might actually be the most consequential pricing decision in the epic. On who makes this call: not our team alone. This needs product, pricing, and finance in the room before we commit. I've been operating like it's a product scope decision, and it's not — it's a business model question with downstream implications for how we count and charge active users. I should have flagged that earlier in the process. The epic shouldn't go to engineering commit without that conversation on record.

Area 8 — Post-Launch Ownership

The retention bet is that stakeholder engagement goes up, which we'd see in renewal and expansion cohorts over time — but that's a lagging indicator and we can't wait six months to know if we're failing. The leading indicator I'd commit to is this: within 60 days of GA, 40% of dashboards that are shared via our share link flow should have at least one comment from a non-analyst role. That tells us stakeholders are actually showing up to participate, not just viewing. If that number is below 20%, the behavior change isn't happening and we have a problem. For beta specifically, I'd want at least two of the three named accounts to have a resolved thread within the first 30 days — resolved meaning someone marked it done, not just that the thread went cold. On telemetry: here's the list as I have it in my head, and I want engineering to pressure-test it. Comment created — with role of creator, chart ID, dashboard ID. Thread resolved — with time-to-resolution and whether the resolver was the original commenter or someone else. @mention sent — with whether the mentioned user had previously visited the dashboard. Notification opened — broken out by email versus in-product, and by role of recipient. Panel opened without commenting — that's a passive engagement signal worth tracking. Comment replied to — with role of replier and time since original comment. Dashboard viewed by a non-analyst after a comment was created — that's the signal that the notification worked and drove a return visit. I don't think we've given this list to engineering yet. Area 9 — Trust, Governance & Auditability

> On the CRUD model: we haven't designed this explicitly and what we have is inconsistent. The working assumption has been that you can edit or delete your own comments, full stop. We haven't defined whether a dashboard owner, a team admin, or a manager can delete someone else's comment. In practice, if an analyst posts something incorrect and their manager wants it removed, there's currently no path for that other than going to a platform admin — and we haven't defined what admin deletion looks like either. For a B2B product this is a real gap. The minimum we need is: comment owner can edit and delete their own, dashboard owner can delete any comment on their dashboard, org admin can delete anything. That's the floor. We haven't written it down. On org-level admin controls: almost entirely unmapped. There is no admin view for comments in the current design. No cross-instance visibility, no export, no audit log. A compliance team receiving a legal hold request today would have no self-serve path to pull comment data — it would require a support escalation and a manual data export from our infrastructure team. That's not acceptable for enterprise accounts, and it's the kind of thing that kills deals in regulated verticals. An audit log isn't optional for that segment. It's a procurement requirement. We should have flagged this before now. On data residency and retention: the 30-day soft delete was a working assumption carried over from our chart data model, not a deliberate policy decision for comments. There is no configurable retention policy. It's fixed, and the customer doesn't control it. For a healthcare or financial services customer under specific retention obligations — some regulations require seven years, some require deletion within a defined window — a fixed 30-day soft delete followed by permanent purge may not be compliant in either direction. We haven't talked to legal about whether comments fall under our existing DPA language or whether they constitute a new data category that needs its own handling. That conversation has to happen before GA, not after. On the blast radius scenario: this is the one that concerns me most. If a stakeholder leaves a comment containing confidential deal information on a dashboard that has an external share link enabled, that comment is currently visible to anyone with the link — including external viewers we may not be able to enumerate. We have no controls to prevent it. There's no way to restrict comments to internal users only on a per-dashboard basis, no warning when someone is about to comment on an externally-shared dashboard, and no way to retroactively scope who saw a comment before it was deleted. The blast radius in that scenario is unbounded from a practical standpoint. Containment requires us to know who accessed the share link and when, which depends on whether we're logging share link views at the user level. We're not, for unauthenticated external viewers. So in the worst case, confidential information is visible to an unknown set of external viewers with no audit trail of who saw it. That scenario tells me we have at minimum two things to add before GA regardless of segment: a per-dashboard setting that restricts commenting to authenticated internal users only, and a clear indication in the UI when a dashboard has external viewers so that commenters understand the audience. Everything else I named — the admin controls, the audit log, the configurable retention — those can be staged to an enterprise tier if we're honest about the tradeoff. But the blast radius scenario is a product-level risk that exists for every customer on every tier, and it needs a control before we ship.
